Welcome to my website !

I’m Simon Oiry, a PhD student at the University of Nantes in France, specializing in the study of intertidal macrophytes. My research focuses on harnessing the power of remote sensing to investigate these captivating coastal ecosystems.

On this website, I will share everything related to my work, including the ecological, scientific, and technical aspects. Here, I share information about the scientific projects I am currently working on and have worked on in the past, as well as the various publications I have been involved in. I will also share all the coding, GIS, and remote sensing experiences I have acquired during my years as a research engineer and doctoral student.

My PhD

The aim of my doctoral research is to map the vegetation that grows in the zone between high tide and low tide, which is completely exposed at low tide (it’s called intertidal zone). This area is primarily inhabited by algae and a few flowering plants. The problem lies in the fact that some of these plants share very similar pigment compositions. This means that they absorb and reflect more or less the same colors (we say they have the same spectral signature). This poses a challenge when attempting to differentiate between these plants using remote sensing because color is the only information that can be retrieved through remote sensing.

Therefore, I am trying to find machine learning methods that can effectively differentiate these plants with similar pigment compositions.

What is remote sensing ?

Remote sensing is a method of gathering data about something without physically being in contact with it. It’s like using satellites or drones to capture information from a distance. This could involve taking pictures (either in the visible or the infrared spectrum), measuring temperature, or even penetrating through clouds and vegetation to understand the terrain (with Lidar or Radar for example). It’s a useful technique for studying the Earth, monitoring the environment, and making informed decisions about things like agriculture, urban development, or environmental management.

In my case, I primarily use remote sensing to observe what is happening in the visible and near-infrared part of the electromagnetic spectrum. It is in these wavelengths that the sun emits the majority of its energy. And it is precisely in these wavelengths that plant pigments absorb light (known as photosynthetically active radiation, or PAR) to carry out photosynthesis.

Spectral resolution

When we talk about remote sensing, one of the most important technical characteristics is what we call spectral resolution. It is so crucial that depending on the spectral resolution, we distinguish between multispectral remote sensing and hyperspectral remote sensing. Spectral resolution basically determines the number of different “colors” (wavelengths) that a sensor measures. Hyperspectral sensors have a very high spectral resolution, but they generate heavy, difficult-to-process, and noisy data. In contrast, multispectral sensors are easier to deploy, generate less data, but have a lower spectral resolution.

In my case, I mainly work with multispectral data because it is the most common type of data in remote sensing. Some products, like Sentinel-2, are freely accessible. When it comes to drones, compared to hyperspectral data, the acquisition and processing of multispectral data are much less demanding. Furthermore, a recent publication has shown that the contribution of hyperspectral data was negligible in accurately discriminating intertidal macrophytes.
